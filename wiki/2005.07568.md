# Graphical modeling of stochastic processes driven by correlated errors (2005.07568)

- **Year**: 2020.05
- **Authors**: Søren Wengel Mogensen (Technical University of Denmark / University of Copenhagen), Niels Richard Hansen (University of Copenhagen)
- **Venue**: arXiv preprint; submission template targets *Bernoulli* (statistics theory journal)
- **Keywords**: local independence graph, mu-separation, directed correlation graph (cDG), Markov equivalence, coNP-completeness, Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process, correlated Brownian motion, global Markov property
- **One-liner**: Extends local independence graphs to correlated driving noise (cDGs), proves a global Markov property for correlated-noise OU processes, and proves deciding Markov equivalence of these graphs is coNP-complete.

## 精华提炼

### 管线/方法
Pure theory paper — no data, no experiments. The "pipeline" is a sequence of definitions and theorems:

1. **Setup**: model an n-dimensional continuous-time stochastic process X as a "regular Itô process" dX_t = λ_t dt + σ dW_t, with a possibly *non-diagonal* diffusion matrix Σ = σσ^T — i.e. the driving noise coordinates of different processes can be correlated. This non-diagonal-Σ generalization is the paper's core departure from prior local-independence work.
2. **Local independence** (Def 2.2, generalizing Didelez 2006/2008 and the authors' own prior DMG work): β is locally independent of α given C if E[λ_t^β | F_t^C] is a version of E[λ_t^β | F_t^{C∪{α}}] — knowing α's history adds nothing to predicting β's drift once C's history is known. A continuous-time analogue of Granger non-causality.
3. **Graph representation — directed correlation graphs (cDGs)**: three edge types — directed (→, direct drift dependence), blunt (⊸, correlated driving noise / "spouses"). Contrasted with the related DMG class (bidirected ↔, representing marginalization/latent confounding instead). cDGs and DMGs are deliberately kept as distinct graph classes because correlated-noise dependence and marginalization dependence are different generative mechanisms that induce different separation structures — the paper proves neither class's independence models are a subset of the other's (Example 3.5/Fig. 3: a cDG whose independence model cannot be represented by any DMG).
4. **μ-separation** (Def 2.10, imported from a companion paper cited as "Mogensen2020a," which introduced it only for DMGs): a walk is μ-connecting from α to β given C if α∉C, every collider is an ancestor of C, no noncollider is in C, and the final edge has a head at β. B is μ-separated from A given C if no μ-connecting walk exists. This paper's contribution is extending the μ-separation machinery to cDGs (and to a common superclass containing both DMGs and cDGs).
5. **Global Markov property (Theorem 3.3)**: for a regular multivariate Ornstein–Uhlenbeck (OU) process (drift λ(x) = M(x−μ), constant diffusion Σ), if B is μ-separated from A given C in the canonical local independence graph, then B is locally independent of A given C in the actual process. Proved via linear filtering theory (Liptser & Shiryaev 1977): the conditional-mean process m_t = E[X_t^U | F_t^W] solves an integral equation driven by a differential Riccati equation; the proof tracks sparsity of that equation's solution through a chain of stabilizability/detectability lemmas (Lyapunov equation → algebraic Riccati equation → differential Riccati equation) to show the relevant conditional expectations don't depend on the separated coordinates.
6. **Markov equivalence characterization (Theorem 4.9, "collider equivalence")**: two cDGs (assumed to contain every self-loop) are Markov equivalent (encode the same μ-separations) iff they have the same directed edges AND are "collider equivalent" — every collider path in one is "covered" by a collider path in the other (covering = every intermediate node is an ancestor of the path's endpoints or of the original path's colliders).
7. **Structural finding**: unlike DMGs (which always have a unique "greatest" Markov-equivalent supergraph, per the authors' earlier result), cDG equivalence classes need NOT have a greatest OR a least element — shown by explicit counterexample (Fig. 5, a 21-member equivalence class). A qualitative complexity jump caused by allowing correlated noise.
8. **Algorithm + cheap necessary condition**: Theorem 5.1 recasts collider-equivalence checking via ancestral sets and their "collider connections," yielding Algorithm 1 (exponential worst case: iterates over ancestral sets of the condensation / strongly-connected-component graph). A cheaper necessary-but-not-sufficient condition uses "virtual collider tripaths" (Def 5.5, Fig. 8 shows two graphs agreeing on all maximal virtual collider tripaths yet disagreeing on an actual μ-separation).
9. **Hardness proof (Theorem 5.9)**: deciding Markov equivalence of cDGs is coNP-complete, via a polynomial many-one reduction from 3DNF-tautology (a canonical coNP-complete problem), adapting a technique from Böhler et al. (2010/2012) on Boolean-circuit-equivalence complexity. Membership in coNP is easy (a separating (A,B,C) certificate is checkable in poly time via the augmentation/moral-graph criterion, Appendix A); hardness is the technical core — a gadget graph is built per literal/variable/term of a 3DNF formula so that a specific 2-edge graph augmentation is Markov-equivalence-preserving iff the formula is a tautology.

### 关键技术细节
- **Process class**: "regular Itô process" — continuous drift λ_t (F_t-adapted), CONSTANT diffusion coefficient σ (n×n invertible, or n×m matrix σ_0 of rank n). Diffusion matrix Σ = σσ^T is fixed for all t — no time-varying Σ(t) or explicitly time-dependent λ(x,t) is ever considered anywhere in the paper.
- **Canonical local independence graph** (Def 2.7): for a regular Itô diffusion with continuously differentiable, autonomous drift λ(x): α→β iff ∂_α λ_β ≠ 0; α⊸β iff Σ_αβ ≠ 0. For the OU special case λ(x) = M(x−μ), so α→β iff M_βα ≠ 0 — a static linear-algebra criterion on a fixed matrix M.
- **Stationarity is invoked only as a side fact, never as a hypothesis of the main theorem**: the paper notes that if M is stable (eigenvalues with negative real parts), a stationary Gaussian distribution N(μ, Γ_∞) exists (Lyapunov equation MΓ_∞ + Γ_∞M^T + Σ = 0). But Theorem 3.3 (the global Markov property) does NOT require M to be stable — it only requires X_0 to be a non-degenerate Gaussian vector with independent entries, independent of the driving BM. So the theorem already covers transient / not-started-at-equilibrium (even explosive, if M is unstable) sample paths, as long as M and Σ themselves stay constant over time.
- **A "stationary case" subsection is drafted in the raw LaTeX source and then wrapped in `\begin{comment}...\end{comment}`, excluded from the actual paper** — only visible by reading the .tex source directly, not in the compiled PDF. It contains an unfinished theorem/proof (with "[FIX: ...]" placeholders) attempting to extend the global Markov property to a doubly-infinite-time stationary process (via Kolmogorov's extension theorem, taking t_0 → −∞). Even this abandoned fragment's notion of "nonstationary" means only "X_0 not drawn from the stationary distribution" (an initial-condition question) — never "M or Σ change over time" (a dynamics question). The authors cut it rather than publish it unfinished.
- **Complexity-reduction gadget**: a 3DNF formula H on n Boolean variables is encoded as a cDG with O(n + literals) nodes — literal nodes ζ, variable/negation nodes χ_l, υ_l chained by blunt edges, with directed-edge/blunt-edge gadgets α→ζ and α→γ_δ↔δ. Adding two extra blunt edges (α⊸χ_1, α⊸υ_1) is Markov-equivalence-preserving iff H is a tautology.
- **Contrast cases cited**: Ali/Richardson/Spirtes 2009 (poly-time Markov-equivalence decision for maximal ancestral graphs) and Richardson 1997 (poly-time for DAGs under d-separation) — i.e., other graph classes admit efficient equivalence checking; cDGs under μ-separation provably do not (assuming P≠coNP).

### 核心结果
- **Theorem 3.3**: global Markov property holds for regular OU processes w.r.t. their canonical local independence (cDG) graph, extending the authors' own diagonal-Σ-only result (UAI 2018) to non-diagonal Σ (correlated driving Brownian motions) — "the first such result in continuous-time models" per the authors, analogous to existing discrete-time (time-series) results by Eichler et al.
- **Theorem 4.9**: Markov equivalence of cDGs (with all self-loops) ⟺ same directed edges + collider equivalence.
- **Fig. 5 example**: a 5-node cDG equivalence class of size 21 that has neither a greatest nor a least element — genuinely new versus DMGs/DAGs/ancestral graphs, which always have a unique maximal (and DAGs a unique) representative.
- **Theorem 5.9**: deciding Markov equivalence of cDGs is coNP-complete (both membership and hardness proved).
- **Separation result**: the independence model of some cDGs cannot be represented by ANY DMG on the same node set (Example 3.5/Fig. 3) — cDGs are a strictly richer / incomparable graph class relative to DMGs, not a subclass or superclass.
- **Counterintuitive point**: maximal cDGs (no edge addable Markov-equivalently) can still have different adjacency sets from each other (Fig. 6) — breaks the usual DAG/ancestral-graph intuition that a Markov equivalence class has a well-defined unique "skeleton."

### 明确局限
- **No estimation, inference, or learning algorithm from data.** Pure identifiability/graph theory. The paper explicitly frames "tests of local independence in observed data" (start of §4) as necessary future empirical work, but does not attempt it.
- **Zero empirical validation** — no simulations, no real data, no experiments, consistent with the target venue (Bernoulli, a statistics theory journal).
- **Global Markov property is proved ONLY for the linear-Gaussian OU case.** The authors explicitly flag (end of §2 intro) that they "conjecture that a generalization... holds for nondiagonal σ as well" for general regular Itô diffusions, "but this cannot be shown using the same techniques." The Conclusion repeats: "It is an open question if and how this can be extended to other or larger classes of continuous-time stochastic processes." So even within the constant-parameter world, the result doesn't cover general nonlinear diffusions with correlated noise.
- **Time-invariance is a blanket, never-questioned assumption**: λ(x) = M(x−μ) with fixed M throughout, Σ fixed throughout. No time-varying M(t), Σ(t), no regime switching, and — critically for this literature search — **no discussion whatsoever of what happens as M approaches instability** (i.e., as its dominant eigenvalue's real part → 0, the bifurcation boundary between stable/damped and unstable/explosive OU dynamics). Never raised as a question, a caveat, or future work — not even in the cut "stationary case" draft section.
- **No discussion of faithfulness.** The paper's whole "Markov equivalence" machinery is the converse concern — characterizing when DIFFERENT graphs encode the SAME independence model — but never asks whether a generic/almost-all parameter choice is faithful to its canonical graph, nor whether faithfulness could degrade measure-theoretically as a parameter (e.g. an entry of M) approaches a non-generic value or a bifurcation manifold. This is a structurally different question from anything in the paper.
- **coNP-completeness is a worst-case result.** The Conclusion notes tractable subclasses likely exist (bounded blunt-component size, bounded blunt-path length) but does not develop them.
- **"Contains every loop" is adopted as a blanket convention** from §3 onward — self-loops are assumed present at every node to simplify the theory; the stated Markov-equivalence theorems don't directly apply verbatim to loop-free graphs without re-derivation.

### 跟我们的关系
For **0710-causal-scs (causal-scs-indicator)**, this paper is the technical engine behind μ-separation but delivers a firmly negative-by-omission result on the round7 question: **it contains zero discussion of non-stationary dynamics, parameter drift, or bifurcation**, and this absence is structural, not incidental.

1. **(a) Non-stationary/bifurcation behavior of local independence — not discussed.** The entire paper, including a section drafted and then deliberately deleted before publication (§3.2 "The stationary case," visible only in the raw LaTeX comment block, not the compiled PDF), treats the dynamics matrices M, Σ as fixed constants. Even the deleted draft's notion of "nonstationary" meant only "X_0 not drawn from the stationary distribution" (an initial-condition question), never "M or Σ change over time" (a dynamics question). The Conclusion's stated open problem is generic ("extend to other/larger classes of continuous-time processes"), not specifically non-stationarity or bifurcation. This corroborates and hardens round6's finding: we have now checked both the μ-separation *origin* engine (via the companion "Mogensen2020a" reference) and its most technically thorough elaboration (this paper), and neither raises time-varying parameters as a question, even hypothetically.

2. **(b) Faithfulness collapse near bifurcation — formally absent, and the wrong tool even in principle.** This paper's "Markov equivalence" concept asks "which different graphs encode the same independence model" — a graph-level redundancy question — not "does a fixed graph's independence model stay faithful to the true process as a parameter (e.g. an eigenvalue of M) approaches a critical value." The proof machinery (Riccati-equation sparsity tracking) is a "which matrix entries are forced to be *exactly* zero" argument — precisely a Markov-property (soundness) proof — and says nothing about near-zero-but-nonzero entries or measure-theoretic genericity/faithfulness. So even the technically closest paper in this whole search does not formalize faithfulness degeneracy.

3. **(c) Stable literature gap, now with mechanism-level backing.** This paper upgrades the round6 finding from "no paper we've read discusses non-stationarity" to "the two most central μ-separation papers (origin + full elaboration) actively assume time-invariant dynamics as a *structural precondition of their proof technique*." The Appendix's Riccati-equation-sparsity argument requires D, E, F — all built from the CONSTANT M, Σ — to define a *single* algebraic Riccati equation with a *single* fixed-point solution Γ̄. If M(t) or Σ(t) varied, there would be no single algebraic Riccati equation to solve at all; the whole sparsity-tracking argument would need to be rebuilt around a time-varying or slowly-converging Riccati flow. This is a mechanism-level argument for why extending this proof technique to bifurcation/parameter-drift settings is not a small technical step — genuinely new machinery would be needed (e.g. tracking sparsity through a Riccati differential equation whose own coefficients approach a singular/non-hyperbolic regime). That is exactly the kind of technical white space that supports novelty for causal-scs-indicator.

4. **Usable tooling.** The μ-separation graphical criterion (Def 2.10) and its "augmentation criterion" decision procedure (Appendix A, reduces separation-checking to moral-graph path-separation) are directly reusable as the *baseline static/fixed-parameter separation oracle* against which a bifurcation-aware or non-stationary extension could be contrasted or benchmarked. The coNP-completeness result (Thm 5.9) is also a useful complexity ceiling to cite: any non-stationary extension of μ-separation inherits at least this worst-case hardness on its fixed-parameter sub-case, so tractability claims for a "drift-aware" indicator should be scoped to structured/bounded cases — mirroring this paper's own suggested tractable subclasses (bounded blunt-component size).

5. **Should be cited**: yes — as the technical fixed-parameter benchmark cDG/μ-separation reference, and as direct evidence (via its own excluded stationary-case draft) that even the paper's authors treated stationarity/non-stationarity as a strictly secondary, initial-condition-only concern, never engaging the bifurcation/drift regime at all.
