# How PC-based Methods Err: Towards Better Reporting of Assumption Violations and Small Sample Errors (2502.14719)

- **Year**: 2025.02
- **Authors**: Sofia Faltenbacher (U. Potsdam, equal contrib.), Jonas Wahl (DFKI, equal contrib.), Rebecca Herman (U. Potsdam), Jakob Runge (U. Potsdam)
- **Venue**: UAI 2026 (accepted)
- **Keywords**: causal discovery, PC algorithm, PCMCI, constraint-based causal discovery, coherency score, conditional independence testing, orientation conflict, faithfulness violation, causal insufficiency, ground-truth-free evaluation
- **One-liner**: Introduces a zero-extra-test "coherency score" that certifies when a PC/PCMCI-family causal-discovery output contradicts its own recorded CI tests, and proves this is the detection ceiling given only that information.

## 精华提炼

### 管线/方法

Input: a dataset $\mathcal{D}$, a PC-based method $\mathcal{M}$ (PC, PC-stable, conservative-PC, majority-PC, FCI, PCMCI/PCMCI+, ...), a CI test $(T,\alpha)$.

1. Run $\mathcal{M}$ on $\mathcal{D}$ as usual. This produces (a) the full log of CI test results $\mathcal{T}=\mathcal{T}^{ind}\cup\mathcal{T}^{dep}$ actually performed during execution, and (b) the output graph $\mathcal{G}_{out}$ (CPDAG for DAG methods, PAG for MAG methods like FCI).
2. For every tested tuple $(X,Y,\mathbf{S})\in\mathcal{T}$, query whether $\mathcal{G}_{out}$'s separation/connection statement (d-separation for CPDAGs, m-separation for PAGs, computable in $\mathcal{O}(n+m)$ via Perković et al. 2015) agrees with the CI test's actual accept/reject decision.
3. A tuple where graph-says-separated-but-test-said-dependent (or vice versa) is an **incoherent tuple**.
4. Aggregate into a **coherency score** $sc(w,\mathcal{M}) = 1-\frac{\sum w(X,Y,\mathbf{S})\cdot\text{inc}(X,Y,\mathbf{S})}{\sum w(X,Y,\mathbf{S})}$, with $w$ a tunable weight function (uniform weight = "total coherency score"; several other weightings are defined, see below). Output is a single number in $[0,1]$, computed purely by replaying the already-recorded test log against the already-computed graph — **no additional CI tests, no ground truth**.
5. Optional diagnostic ("increasing subsets" heuristic, Appendix): compute the score on the full dataset and on nested increasing subsets ($n=50,100,1000,10000,\dots$). If the score trends to 1 as $n$ grows, the incoherence looks like finite-sample/statistical error; if it plateaus below 1 across sample sizes, it looks like a structural assumption violation.
6. Optional negative-control heuristic: generate $K$ Erdős–Rényi random graphs at the same edge density $p$ as $\mathcal{G}_{out}$, simulate data respecting $\mathcal{M}$'s assumptions, rerun $\mathcal{M}$+score on each, and flag the real result if its score is worse than $(1-\beta)\cdot100\%$ of the null instances.

### 关键技术细节

- Central conceptual move: replace the (unavailable) comparison against ground truth $\mathcal{G}_{true}$ ("erroneous tuple") with a **self-consistency check against the method's own output** ("incoherent tuple"). This is the only thing the score needs.
- Formal results (proofs in Appendix A):
  - **Lemma**: if there is no graph-type mismatch, an incoherent tuple implies an erroneous tuple — i.e. incoherence never gives a false alarm about CI-deviance.
  - **Proposition (G3 = D3)**: "no conflicts/ambiguities/incoherencies" (Case-G3, graph level) is exactly equivalent to "a distribution exists that is Markovian+faithful to $\mathcal{G}_{out}$ and matches all CI tests" (Case-D3, distribution level). This *is* the theoretical detection ceiling from (CI-test log + output graph) alone.
  - **Corollary**: resolving orientation conflicts or ambiguities (p-value ranking, keep-first/keep-last, majority rule) always introduces incoherencies elsewhere — conflict resolution hides evidence of error from the graph's surface but the coherency score can still see it.
- Score family beyond the uniform-weight total score: Independence-Connection-Mismatch (ICM, weight only on tested-independent tuples), Dependence-Separation-Mismatch (DSM, weight only on graph-separated tuples), and conditioning-set-size- or collider-free-path-length-adjusted variants that down-weight tuples most likely to carry small, hard-to-detect true effects.
- Complexity: $\mathcal{O}((n+m)\cdot|\mathcal{T}|)$ total. Empirically <1s to score an 8-variable instance; 8 minutes to score all 104,000 simulated graphs from the SHD study (vs. 24h to generate them by running parallel PC).
- Implementation: `causy` Python package (parallelized, order-independent PC), Fisher's Z test, $\alpha=0.05$, benchmarked on a MacBook Pro M2 Pro / 32GB.
- Real datasets: Auto-MPG (6 vars, 392 samples, UCI) — total coherency score 0.917 despite **zero orientation conflicts** in the default `dowhy`-documented PC configuration. Sachs protein-signaling data (11 vars, Sachs et al. 2005) — orientation conflicts present; naive "keep-first" resolution gives score 0.98.
- Large synthetic sweep: 10,400 random DAGs (4–9 nodes, edge weights in [0.8, 1.2], 1000 samples each) run through parallel PC; mean total coherency score tracks (inversely) the SHD between the output CPDAG and the ground-truth CPDAG.
- Assumption-violation simulations: four SCM families (unconfounded → moderate → strong → dense confounding via hidden $U_1..U_4$). Confounded/causally-insufficient models plateau at a lower score essentially flat across sample size 50–10,000; the unconfounded model's score → 1 as $n$ grows. Dense confounding is flagged as an explicit failure edge case (full confounding ⟹ fully-connected output graph ⟹ empty $\mathcal{T}^{ind}$ ⟹ nothing left to be incoherent about).
- Method-selection demo: on a 4-hidden-confounder SCM, oracle-FCI reaches coherency score 1 while oracle-PC caps at $15/16$ under any conflict-resolution strategy — usable as an automatic, ground-truth-free criterion for preferring FCI over PC when causal insufficiency is suspected.

### 核心结果

- Auto-MPG: an ambiguity in the $mpg$–$horsepower$–$displacement$ unshielded triple resolves either way to total coherency score 0.9174 — proves a textbook "clean" PC run (no visible conflicts) is nonetheless provably CI-deviant or graph-type-mismatched.
- Sachs: orientation conflicts present; naive resolution → score 0.98 (high but explicitly flagged as needing further interpretation, since high score is necessary, not sufficient, for correctness).
- SHD-proxy result (10,400-DAG sweep): mean total coherency score empirically tracks mean SHD-to-ground-truth — first empirical demonstration that a ground-truth-free score is a usable proxy for structural error.
- Assumption-violation result: confounded models plateau at a lower score independent of sample size while the unconfounded model's score converges to 1 — the empirical signature the paper proposes for telling apart "will improve with more data" (statistical error) vs. "won't improve with more data" (structural violation), using nothing but increasing subsets of one dataset.
- Mediated-effect experiment ($c=0.2,1,10$, i.e. varying total effect strength): for a small true effect ($c=0.2$, total effect $\approx0.04$), even $n=10{,}000$ samples yields a **coherent-but-wrong** output (score ≈1 despite a real missing edge) — a concrete instance of the paper's own "undetectable erroneous output" theorem.
- Oracle method-selection: oracle-FCI score 1 vs. oracle-PC score $15/16$ on the same causal-insufficiency SCM.

### 明确局限

- Score $<1$ proves an error exists, but the output graph can still be Markov-equivalent to the (unknown) true graph. Score $=1$ does **not** prove correctness — undetectable erroneous outputs provably exist (Case-D3/G3 theorem), and the paper shows a concrete instance (small mediated effect, $c=0.2$).
- No calibrated statistical threshold for "how low is too low" — only heuristics (increasing-subset trend-watching; Erdős–Rényi-matched negative control), explicitly not a formal test with known Type-I error.
- Score loses power under dense/heavy confounding — an explicitly named edge case where full confounding drives the output to a fully-connected graph with nothing left to check for incoherence, even though the output is maximally wrong.
- Scope is constraint-based/CI-test-driven methods only; explicitly does **not** cover score-based methods (e.g. GES) — contrasted against Faller et al.'s self-compatibility check, which does cover score-based methods but at much higher (super-exponential-in-subset) cost.
- No real ground-truth validation on real data: Auto-MPG and Sachs results are reported without a known-correct answer, so the paper can only say "this dataset+method combo is provably imperfect," not "here is the true error rate."
- PCMCI/time-series causal discovery is named as an in-scope method $\mathcal{M}$ and time-series robustness work is cited (Miersch et al. 2025), but the paper's own experiments are all i.i.d./tabular or static simulated data — **no time-series, autocorrelated, or nonstationary experiment is actually run** in this paper.
- No code-release link for the authors' own scoring implementation appears in the read source (`causy` is a third-party package used for the base PC runs, not the scoring code itself).

### 跟我们的关系

**No collision with the core claim.** `0710-causal-scs`'s bottom-line hypothesis is that *instability in rolling causal-effect estimates is a pre-event diagnostic of severe-convective-system onset* — a temporal, early-warning-signal framing. 2502.14719 does not do time-series early warning or tipping-point detection at all; it is a single-run, ground-truth-free error/assumption-violation certificate for one causal-discovery output, evaluated on i.i.d. tabular data (Auto-MPG, Sachs) and static random-DAG simulations. There is no overlap to disclose or defend against on the central claim.

**Directly reusable tool for the project's own bottleneck.** The paper's "increasing subsets" heuristic (Appendix, *Further Heuristics and Tests*) is close to a structural match for the project's stated "must-solve bottleneck": *separate event-specific instability from estimator noise, ordinary nonstationarity, finite-window effects*. The paper's version: compute the coherency score on the full dataset and on nested increasing subsets; convergence to 1 with more data ⟹ looks like statistical/small-sample error, plateau below 1 ⟹ looks like a structural assumption violation. This is a ready-made template for a signal/noise separator, not yet a rolling/pre-event construct as-is.

**Concretely actionable at the project's own next step.** `EXPERIMENT_LOG.md` (2026-07-11 run) explicitly states the next step is to rerun the two-channel metric with **PCMCI+ and LKIF** — and PCMCI is named by 2502.14719 itself as an in-scope "PC-based method" $\mathcal{M}$. Once PCMCI+ runs are in the pipeline, each rolling window's PCMCI+ CI-test log + output graph could feed the coherency score at **zero extra compute** (it only replays tests PCMCI+ already performed). A candidate third channel: track whether coherency degrades specifically in the pre-event window relative to matched paired controls — either as its own channel, or folded into the existing dispersion/jump framing (a coherency drop reads as jump-like; a sustained low plateau reads as dispersion-like).

**A relevant methodological precedent already validates part of the project's own null-calibration design.** The paper's negative-control heuristic (simulate $K$ Erdős–Rényi graphs matched on edge density, reject if the real score underperforms $(1-\beta)\%$ of the null distribution) is the same move as the pilot's own "method-specific null percentiles, learned only from calibration controls" — a matched-density random-graph null vs. the pilot's matched-trajectory null. Worth citing as independent convergence on the same idea, and possibly cross-checking calibration approaches against it.

**A cautionary parallel worth carrying forward.** The paper's own related-work review reports that Gamella et al. 2025 (Causal Chambers) found PCMCI+ "exhibits low recall and performs similar to random guessing" on a real wind-tunnel testbed with weak nonlinear effects — a direct echo of `0710-causal-scs`'s own NEGATIVE pilot result (AUROC 0.39–0.52 for the linear-VAR proxy). This pre-emptively flags a real risk for the "run PCMCI+ next" plan: PCMCI+ may itself underperform on weak/nonlinear real atmospheric effects, independent of whether the instability-as-EWS hypothesis is true. Miersch et al. 2025 (PCMCI+ robustness for flood drivers — real, nonstationary, geophysical time series) is the closest real-world precedent for applying PCMCI+-family causal discovery to an environmental early-warning-adjacent problem and is a strong deep-lit-read candidate for the next round.

**Boundary of applicability.** The score is specific to CI-test/constraint-based estimators, so it can only ever cover the PCMCI+ channel of the two planned real-data estimators — it does not apply to LKIF (Liang-Kleeman Information Flow), which is not CI-test-based. Any adoption also requires re-deriving how the score is computed and calibrated *per rolling window* and how it composes with the project's existing paired-control bootstrap design, since the source paper only ever scores one static output, never a rolling sequence.

**Differentiation if adopted.** 2502.14719's coherency score is a graph-level self-consistency check, not an early-warning signal by itself. The novel, unaddressed-by-this-paper move for `0710-causal-scs` would be tracking the coherency score's *trajectory* across a rolling pre-event window (not just its instantaneous value) and testing whether that trajectory — rather than the raw score — discriminates transitions from matched non-tipping nonstationarity. That framing is untouched by this paper and would be a legitimate delta.
