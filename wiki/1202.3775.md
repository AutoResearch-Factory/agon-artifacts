# Kernel-based Conditional Independence Test and Application in Causal Discovery (1202.3775)

## 精华提炼

### 管线/方法
Input → i.i.d. continuous samples of X, Y, and conditioning set Z (any of these can be multivariate).

1. Build kernel matrices with a characteristic kernel (Gaussian RBF by default) for `Ẍ ≜ (X,Z)`, for `Y`, and for `Z`; centralize each via `H = I − (1/n)·11ᵀ` to get `K̃`.
2. Regress each of `Ẍ` and `Y` on `Z` using kernel ridge regression, giving the "residualizing" operator `R_Z = ε(K̃_Z + εI)⁻¹`.
3. Form the conditional (residualized) kernel matrices `K̃_(Ẍ|Z) = R_Z K̃_Ẍ R_Z` and `K̃_(Y|Z) = R_Z K̃_Y R_Z` — these represent the parts of `Ẍ` and `Y` that cannot be explained by `Z`.
4. Compute the test statistic `T_CI = (1/n)·Tr(K̃_(Ẍ|Z) · K̃_(Y|Z))`.
5. Under H0 (X ⊥⊥ Y | Z), derive that `T_CI` has the same asymptotic distribution as a weighted sum of i.i.d. χ²₁ variables, with weights = eigenvalues of a matrix built from the eigen-decompositions of the two conditional kernel matrices.
6. Approximate that null distribution two ways: (a) Monte Carlo simulation of the weighted χ² sum, or (b) a two-parameter Gamma(k,θ) fit matched by moments, with `k, θ` given in closed form from traces of kernel-matrix products (no simulation needed — this is the practical, deployed version).
7. p-value = P(null-distributed statistic > observed T_CI); reject CI if p-value < α.
8. The same machinery specializes to an **unconditional** independence test `T_UI = (1/n)·Tr(K̃_X K̃_Y)` (statistically identical to the HSIC biased estimator of Gretton et al. 2008, but with a different, simpler derivation of its asymptotic null).
9. Application layer: swap KCI-test in as the conditional-independence oracle inside the constraint-based **PC algorithm** (Spirtes et al. 2001) to recover a DAG's Markov equivalence class from purely continuous, nonlinear, non-Gaussian data — no discretization, no assumption of linear-Gaussian relationships.

### 关键技术细节
- **Kernel choice**: Gaussian RBF, a characteristic kernel — required so that "zero conditional cross-covariance operator" is equivalent to true conditional independence (Fukumizu et al. 2004/2008 characterization, Lemma 1 in the paper).
- **Kernel-width heuristics**: for the unconditional test, variables normalized to unit variance, then width fixed by sample size: 0.8 if n≤200, 0.3 if n>1200, 0.5 otherwise (paper reports this simple schedule "always works well" in their experiments).
- **Conditioning-set regression hyperparameters** (ε, and the kernel widths for `K̃_Z`): when dim(Z) is 1–2, fixed empirical constants suffice (ε=1e−3, kernel width = half of the `K̃_Ẍ`/`K̃_Y` width). When dim(Z) is larger, the paper extends **Gaussian Process regression to a multi-output setting** and learns separate `{ε_f, σ_f}` and `{ε_g′, σ_g′}` by maximizing total marginal likelihood — this is the mechanism that keeps the regression step from over/under-fitting as the conditioning set grows.
- **Numerical practicality**: eigenvalues below 1e−5 are dropped everywhere (in `K̃_(Ẍ|Z)`, `K̃_(Y|Z)`, and the derived weight matrix) to keep the weighted-χ² computation tractable.
- **Complexity**: eigendecomposition-based, effectively O(n³) per test; the paper reports orders-of-magnitude lower CPU time than the CIPERM baseline's 500-bootstrap procedure, especially as conditioning-set dimension D grows — though KCI-test's own cost is more sensitive to n (dominated by GP hyperparameter learning) and comparatively insensitive to D.
- **Theoretical base**: a new general theorem (Theorem 3) gives the asymptotic distribution of any kernel-matrix-trace statistic under an "uncorrelated-in-RKHS" null; both the unconditional (HSIC-equivalent) test and the conditional test are derived as special cases of this one theorem — this generality is the paper's main theoretical contribution, not just the CI test itself.
- **Baseline compared throughout**: CIPERM, the local-permutation/bootstrap CI test built on Fukumizu et al. (2008)'s conditional-dependence measure (500 bootstrap samples per test, no known asymptotic null so it must resample every time); a secondary check also compares to plain partial correlation in the linear-Gaussian special case.
- **Simulated CI-test evaluation design**: post-nonlinear generative model `X = G(F(Z)+E)` (per Zhang & Hyvärinen 2009); Case I (only one of D conditioning variables is actually effective) vs. Case II (all D are effective); D=1..5, n=200/400, 1000 replications, α=0.01/0.05 — both Type I and Type II error rates reported.
- **Simulated causal-discovery evaluation**: random 4-node DAGs (edges present w.p. 0.5, only i<j allowed), structural functions drawn from a Gaussian-Process prior (linear parent term + GP kernel + noise kernel), n=100..700, 100 DAGs per setting, α=0.01 — metric is the fraction of runs recovering the exact correct Markov equivalence class.
- **Real-data application**: UCI Boston Housing dataset (continuous variables only), α=0.001 chosen ad hoc "as a rough way to correct for multiple testing" — compares the skeletons found by PC+KCI-test vs. PC+CIPERM.
- **Code**: MATLAB source released at publication (`http://people.tuebingen.mpg.de/kzhang/KCI-test.zip`, link is legacy/likely dead now); the method has since been independently re-implemented in widely-used open-source toolboxes (e.g. `causal-learn`'s `kci`, `pcalg`'s `kernelCItest`) well beyond this paper's own artifact.

### 核心结果
- **Type I error control**: with the exact (simulated) null distribution, empirical Type I error rates track the nominal α (0.01/0.05) closely across D=1..5 and n=200/400. The Gamma approximation is slightly conservative (produces marginally higher Type-I rates than nominal), consistent with the true null having a slightly heavier tail than a two-parameter Gamma can capture.
- **Type II error** grows monotonically with conditioning-set dimension D (expected curse-of-dimensionality effect on finite-sample power) but shrinks quickly as n goes from 200 → 400.
- **CIPERM comparison (the key motivating result)**: CIPERM's Type I error already exceeds the nominal level at D=1 and degrades sharply as D grows; for D>3 its Type II error rockets toward 1 — i.e. CIPERM essentially always fails to detect real dependence once the conditioning set is even moderately large. KCI-test does not show this collapse.
- **CPU time**: KCI-test is orders of magnitude faster than CIPERM's 500-bootstrap procedure as D grows (log-scale comparison, Fig. 2b).
- **PC-algorithm recovery rate** (4-node random DAGs, GP-generated data, n=100–700): PC+KCI-test has clearly the highest probability of recovering the exact correct Markov equivalence class, beating both PC+CIPERM and PC+partial-correlation.
- **Boston Housing skeleton**: PC+KCI-test recovers substantively plausible links that PC+CIPERM / Margaritis (2005)'s discretization method miss (e.g. IND↔NOX, non-retail business acreage vs. nitric-oxide concentration), while avoiding a spurious CRI→NOX (crime rate → nitric oxide) link that the discretization-based method produces.
- **Honest/surprising secondary finding**: in the special linear-Gaussian case, plain partial correlation still gives *smaller* Type II errors than KCI-test — the paper explicitly frames this as "the price we pay for generality," since KCI-test is built for the fully nonparametric nonlinear/non-Gaussian regime and isn't claimed to beat a correctly-specified linear test on its own turf.

### 明确局限
- **No ground-truth causal graph** for the real-data (Boston Housing) validation — the paper is explicit that edges are judged only by qualitative plausibility, not a known DAG ("one can argue about the ground truth for this data set").
- Causal-discovery results **inherit all of PC's known fragility**: faithfulness assumption, and sensitivity of the final graph to early-stage CI-test errors — the paper flags this as a structural weakness that a better CI test alone cannot fix.
- **Hyperparameter tuning overhead**: fixed heuristics only work when dim(Z) is small; larger conditioning sets require fitting a multi-output GP regression by marginal-likelihood maximization for every test — added tuning cost that plain partial-correlation tests don't have.
- **No interventional/RCT-validated benchmark anywhere** in the paper — all causal-discovery evidence is either simulated DAGs (with known ground truth by construction) or the single Boston Housing dataset (with no known ground truth at all).
- The method is a **CI-testing primitive only**: it does not orient edges, does not handle latent confounders or selection bias, and is not a complete causal-discovery algorithm by itself — it must be embedded inside something like PC, which introduces PC's own limitations.
- **Scalability is not discussed**: the largest example has n≤700 and ≤13 variables (Boston Housing); the O(n³)-ish eigendecomposition cost per test, multiplied by the many tests PC issues over growing conditioning sets, is not analyzed for larger n or many-variable regimes. (This exact gap is what the 2025 FastKCI follow-up, below, later addresses directly.)
- **Single-run real-data result**: the Boston Housing significance level (0.001) is chosen ad hoc, and there is no stability/bootstrap analysis of the discovered skeleton itself, nor replication across multiple real datasets.

### 跟我们的关系
This is the original, still-standard kernel-based nonparametric CI test used throughout the causal-discovery literature. For **0710-causal-scs**, the relevance is as **infrastructure/baseline, not competing prior art on an indicator claim**:
- If the SCS indicator work needs a general-purpose nonlinear/non-Gaussian CI-testing subroutine (e.g., to test a candidate conditional independence implied by a proposed causal/spurious-correlation structure), KCI-test — or a modern re-implementation (`causal-learn`'s `kci`, `pcalg`'s `kernelCItest`) — is the natural off-the-shelf tool to cite/reuse rather than re-derive from scratch.
- It does **not** compete with a "score/indicator for detecting spurious correlation" claim: KCIT is a hypothesis test for conditional independence, not a scoring method, and it doesn't itself address confounding, selection bias, or produce a causal orientation. A spurious-correlation indicator built on top of CI testing should cite KCIT as the underlying primitive but must differentiate on the indicator/scoring construction, not on the CI test itself.
- The paper's own flagged gaps — curse of dimensionality in the conditioning set, no confounder/orientation handling, GP-tuning overhead, no scalability analysis — are exactly the kind of "everyone assumes CI testing just works" gaps a new indicator paper could point to, but they are well-trodden ground by now (see FastKCI 2025 below), so differentiation must go further than just naming these gaps.
- **Overlap risk is low** unless 0710-causal-scs's core mechanism *is itself* a new conditional-independence test — if so, this paper plus the substantial modern kernel-CI-test literature (HSIC, generalized covariance measure, sequential/anytime-valid CI testing — see 反引文 below) would need explicit claim-level differentiation.
