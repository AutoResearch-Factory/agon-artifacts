# The robustness of differentiable causal discovery in misspecified scenarios (2510.12503)

- **Year**: 2025.10 (arXiv submission; LaTeX uses `iclr2025_conference.sty` with `\iclrfinalcopy` active, i.e. formatted as an ICLR 2025 camera-ready — but the arXiv timestamp is Oct 2025, well after ICLR 2025's actual camera-ready window, so the true peer-review venue/status cannot be confirmed from the source alone)
- **Authors**: Huiyang Yi¹, Yanyan He² (equal contribution), Duxin Chen¹ (corresponding author), Mingyu Kang², He Wang¹, Wenwu Yu¹ — ¹School of Mathematics, Southeast University, Nanjing, China; ²School of Cyber Science and Engineering, Southeast University, Nanjing, China
- **Venue**: arXiv preprint (ICLR-2025-styled template; see year note above)
- **Keywords**: causal discovery, differentiable/gradient-based causal discovery, structure learning, model assumption violation, robustness benchmark, Structural Hamming Distance (SHD), Structural Intervention Distance (SID), Var-Sortability, noise-ratio identifiability
- **One-liner**: A 70,000+-experiment benchmark of 12 causal discovery algorithms under 8 assumption-violation scenarios finds differentiable (gradient-based) methods robust everywhere except scale variation, and explains why via a noise-ratio identifiability argument.

## 精华提炼

### 管线/方法

This is a pure empirical-benchmark paper (no new algorithm proposed) — the "pipeline" is a data-generation → algorithm-battery → metric pipeline run at scale:

1. **Base ("vanilla") data-generating models.** Two SCM families are defined as the clean baseline, both satisfying identifiability and the assumptions of most benchmark methods:
   - *Linear vanilla*: coefficients ~ U(−2,−0.5) ∪ U(0.5,2), additive standard-Gaussian noise (settings from Zheng et al. 2018, NOTEARS).
   - *Nonlinear vanilla*: SEM drawn from a Gaussian process with an RBF kernel (bandwidth 1), additive standard-Gaussian noise (settings from Zheng et al. 2020, NOTEARS-MLP). A third "MLP vanilla" variant (Gaussian-process mechanism replaced by a 1-hidden-layer, 100-unit MLP) is used specifically to give CAM a *less* favorable mechanism match (see Core Results).
2. **Eight misspecification scenarios** applied on top of either vanilla model (each violates one commonly-unverifiable causal assumption):
   - **Heterogeneous** — two "domains" e1/e2 mixed at proportion P1 ∈ {0.1,...,0.9}, with noise variance in e2 scaled by γ ∈ {0.01,...,0.5} (same causal graph, different noise scale — a stand-in for non-stationary/multi-domain data).
   - **Scale-variant** — each variable standardized to zero-mean/unit-variance post-hoc; ground-truth graph unchanged.
   - **Missing** — MCAR (Missing Completely At Random) with probability β ∈ {0.005,...,0.1}; rows with any missing value are dropped, then re-padded to preserve sample count.
   - **Mechanism violation** — feed nonlinear-generated data to linear-only algorithms and vice versa.
   - **Latent confounders, measurement error, unfaithful distributions, autoregressive effects** — these four reuse the exact scenario definitions from Montagna et al. 2024 (closest prior benchmark) rather than being newly designed; the paper's novel contribution here is running *differentiable* methods through them, which Montagna et al. did not.
3. **Graph/sample generation.** ER (Erdős–Rényi) and SF (scale-free) graphs at d ∈ {10, 20, 50} nodes, average degree k ∈ {2, 4} (plus ER-6 and a Gaussian-Random-Partition graph at degree 6, both only at d=20); 10 random seeds × 2000 samples per (scenario, graph-type, mechanism) cell. Total: >70,000 experiments across 2,400+ synthetic datasets.
4. **12 benchmark algorithms** spanning 4 families: constraint-based (PC), score-based (GES), functional-causal-model-based (DirectLiNGAM, CAM, Var-SortnRegress, R²-SortnRegress), and differentiable/gradient-based (NOTEARS, GOLEM, NOTEARS-MLP, GraN-DAG, NoCurl, DAGMA) — plus a `Random` graph baseline. Each algorithm uses its original authors' public implementation (or `causal-learn`/`gCastle`/Causal Discovery Toolbox for the classical ones).
5. **Hyperparameter selection**: sparsity coefficient λ1 ∈ {0.005, 0.01, 0.05, 0.5, 2, 5} for NOTEARS/GOLEM/NOTEARS-MLP/DAGMA, significance level α ∈ {0.001, 0.01, 0.05, 0.1} for PC/CAM/GraN-DAG — **the reported number for each method-dataset cell is the best score over this grid, i.e. oracle-tuned against the (otherwise inaccessible) ground-truth graph.** This is stated explicitly ("the hyperparameters for each method are determined as the optimal values relative to the specific dataset") but is a first-order caveat on every headline number (see 明确局限).
6. **Metrics**: SHD (edge insert/delete/reverse count vs. true graph — structural distance) and SID (intervention-distribution distance vs. true graph — causal-ordering distance); for CPDAG-output methods (PC, GES) undirected edges are scored favorably as correctly-directed, following Zheng et al. 2018's convention.
7. **Extra robustness probes beyond the 8-scenario grid** (appendix only): 2/3/4 *combined* misspecifications simultaneously; non-Gaussian (exponential) vanilla noise; *extreme* measurement error (δ=10, vs. the main-grid δ ≤ 0.8); the real-world Sachs protein-signaling dataset (11 nodes, 17 edges, 7,466 samples); semi-synthetic data generated on the Sachs graph topology; and a runtime comparison.

### 关键技术细节

- **Differentiable-method formulations covered** (all cast as `min F(G;X) s.t. h(W(G))=0`, solved via the augmented-Lagrangian method): NOTEARS (least-squares + ℓ1, `h=Tr(e^{W∘W})−d`); GOLEM (max-likelihood with equal/non-equal-variance variants GOLEM-EV/NV, replacing the hard DAG constraint with a soft log-penalty term, provably avoiding NOTEARS's cyclic-graph failure mode in the equal-variance case); NOTEARS-MLP (one small MLP per variable, edge weight = L2 norm of the relevant first-layer weight column); GraN-DAG (per-variable NN + a depth-independent connection-matrix construction, max-likelihood under the additive-noise-model assumption); NoCurl (DAG reparameterized as a skew-symmetric matrix + a potential function via `A = W∘ReLU(grad(p))`, avoiding the ALM's iterative optimization entirely); DAGMA (`h^s_ldet(W) = −log det(sI−W∘W) + d log s`, proved to be a tighter upper bound on both NOTEARS's and DAG-GNN's acyclicity penalties, with a gradient that avoids the vanishing-gradient issue the other two exhibit and a log-det/matrix-inverse computation that is empirically the fastest of the three).
- **Theoretical explanation (Section 3.5.3), built on Loh & Bühlmann 2014's Theorems 7 & 9**: for a linear SEM with least-squares scoring, define the noise ratio `r = max(σ_i²)/min(σ_i²)` across all d variables. Theorem 7: if noise variances are exactly equal (r=1), minimizing least-squares recovers the true DAG in the large-sample limit. Theorem 9: recovery is still guaranteed if `r < 1 + ξ/d`, where ξ is the score gap between the true DAG and the next-best DAG. The paper then derives, for each scenario, how r moves away from 1:
  - *Measurement error*: r inflates because marginal variance increases with causal depth (a known finding of Reisach et al. 2021) — added-noise variance is proportional to `Var(X_i)`, so downstream (higher-variance) nodes get proportionally more corruption, spreading the noise-variance range.
  - *Unfaithful (path-cancellation) model*: worked 3-node example shows that after a triplet's direct-effect cancellation, the affected node's noise term becomes `U_k − U_j` with variance 2 (vs. 1 elsewhere), so r jumps from 1 to 2 deterministically, regardless of graph size.
  - *Missing (MCAR)*: row-deletion-then-repad leaves the i.i.d. assumption and r=1 intact — this is offered as the explanation for why differentiable methods show *no* degradation here, unlike the other two.
  - No equivalent theorem is given for the *nonlinear* differentiable methods (NOTEARS-MLP, GraN-DAG, DAGMA-nonlinear) — their robustness pattern is observed empirically only; the paper explicitly flags this as a gap ("there has been a notable absence of research investigating the performance of nonlinear methods … in the context of scale variation").
- **CAM vs. differentiable methods — a methodological control.** The nonlinear-vanilla model's Gaussian-process mechanism happens to match CAM's own modeling assumption, so CAM looks best in the main nonlinear tables. To check whether this is an artifact, the paper reruns everything with the mechanism swapped to a 1-hidden-layer MLP (Section 3.5.1/"discussion cam") — under this less-CAM-favorable mechanism, NOTEARS-MLP wins almost every scenario except scale variation, supporting the claim that CAM's apparent edge was partly a matched-mechanism artifact rather than genuine robustness superiority.
- **Runtime** (Table in Appendix D, d=10/20, seconds, mean±std over 10 reps): DAGMA 2.41±0.32 / 3.19±0.48 — cheapest differentiable method; NOTEARS-MLP 4.70±0.82 / 5.84±0.97; NOTEARS 9.35±2.63 / 35.57±4.71; NoCurl 5.68±0.49 / 10.29±1.84; CAM 45.73±2.67 / 113.37±3.85; GOLEM 130.23±2.54 / 178.52±3.41; GraN-DAG 119.28±2.15 / 211.59±4.35. I.e. the best-performing differentiable methods are also ~20–50× cheaper than CAM/GOLEM/GraN-DAG.
- **Sachs real-data setup**: 11 nodes (canonical protein-signaling network), 17 ground-truth edges, 7,466 samples, all 12 methods run with the same hyperparameter-grid protocol as the synthetic experiments.
- **Semi-synthetic data**: reuses the Sachs *graph topology* (11 nodes) but regenerates the *data* from the linear/MLP vanilla-model equations plus all 8 misspecification scenarios — a middle ground between purely synthetic and purely real.

### 核心结果

| Scenario (ER-2, 10 nodes, linear) | Best method | SHD | Best traditional | SHD |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Vanilla | DAGMA | 1.2±1.2 | PC | 12.4±3.1 |
| Latent confounders | GOLEM | 6.7±2.8 | GES | 25.9±7.7 |
| Measurement error | NoCurl | 11.8±1.8 | PC | 19.4±4.1 |
| Autoregressive | NOTEARS/DAGMA | 12.2±3.6 | PC | 14.5±2.0 |
| Heterogeneous | NOTEARS/DAGMA | 5.5±2.7/2.3 | GES | 15.5±6.1 |
| Unfaithful | GOLEM/DAGMA | 2.1±2.2 | PC | 13.9±3.2 |
| **Scale-variant** | **PC** | **12.4±3.1** | (differentiable methods 17.5–27.2, i.e. *worse*) | — |
| Missing | DAGMA | 1.5±1.4 | GES | 10.1±5.2 |
| Mechanism violation | Var-SortnRegress | 15.6±3.3 | (differentiable 18.6–19.1, close but not best) | — |

- Scale variation is the **one** scenario where linear differentiable methods (NOTEARS, GOLEM, NoCurl, DAGMA) fall *below* PC/GES — replicates Reisach et al. 2021's finding for linear methods, and the paper reports this is the first time the same degradation is shown empirically for *nonlinear* differentiable methods too (NOTEARS-MLP, GraN-DAG, DAGMA-nonlinear all underperform their non-scale-variant numbers, though — unlike the linear case — they still generally beat PC/GES in the nonlinear setting).
- **Sachs real-data table** (11 nodes, 17 edges): DAGMA best overall, SHD=12, SID=42; next-best DirectLiNGAM/NOTEARS-MLP/GraN-DAG cluster around SHD 14–15; Random baseline SHD=33. Paper argues Sachs is itself a known real-world *heterogeneous* dataset, so this is read as external validation of the heterogeneity-robustness finding.
- **Combined misspecifications** (2/3/4 stacked simultaneously, ER-2/10 nodes): all methods degrade further as more violations stack (e.g. DAGMA SHD rises from 1.2 vanilla → 12.0 → 14.9 → 17.9 across two/three/four combined), but differentiable methods retain their relative ranking advantage throughout.
- **Extreme measurement error appendix (δ=10, vs. main-grid δ≤0.8)** — the one result that most complicates the headline "robust" claim: at d=10, DAGMA's SHD *worsens* from 1.2 (vanilla) to 20.7 under δ=10, becoming *worse* than PC (19.2) and roughly tied with DirectLiNGAM (20.0); at d=20 the ordering flips again, with DAGMA (49.4) now worse than DirectLiNGAM (41.8), PC (44.5), and even Random-adjacent GES (51.2 — barely better than DAGMA). The paper is explicit that this is because the noise ratio r keeps growing with δ, eventually breaking the `r < 1+ξ/d` recovery condition entirely — i.e. "robust to measurement error" only holds for the mild δ range actually swept in the main grid.
- Non-Gaussian (exponential) vanilla noise and semi-synthetic (Sachs-topology) experiments both reproduce the same qualitative pattern (differentiable-robust-except-scale-variation) as the Gaussian synthetic grid.

### 明确局限

- **Oracle hyperparameter tuning.** Every headline number is the best score over a hand-picked λ1/α grid, selected *against the ground-truth graph* — a form of information leakage that is disclosed but not foregrounded as a limitation. Real deployments cannot do this (ground truth is exactly what's unknown), so the practical robustness claim is weaker than the raw numbers suggest; this is the single biggest caveat on the paper's own headline claim, and the authors do not run a "single fixed hyperparameter across all scenarios" ablation to show how much of the "robustness" survives without oracle tuning.
- **Four of eight scenarios (confounding, measurement error, unfaithful, autoregressive) are not new** — they're imported unchanged from Montagna et al. 2024's scenario definitions. The paper's actual novel coverage is: differentiable methods run through *all eight* (Montagna et al. only tested traditional/score-matching methods), plus the four newly-designed scenarios (heterogeneous, scale-variant, missing, mechanism-violation).
- **No theory for nonlinear-method robustness** — the noise-ratio argument only covers linear least-squares differentiable methods; NOTEARS-MLP/GraN-DAG/DAGMA-nonlinear robustness is asserted from tables only, explicitly flagged by the authors as an open research gap.
- **Real-world validation is a single 11-node dataset** (Sachs) — same limitation the causal-discovery field has had for two decades; the semi-synthetic Sachs-topology experiments mitigate this only partially (data is still simulated).
- **The "robust except scale variation" headline undersells the extreme-δ appendix result** — the paper's abstract claims robustness "in commonly used challenging scenarios," which is technically true (the main-grid δ range is mild) but the appendix shows this breaks down entirely at larger, still-plausible measurement-error magnitudes; a reader who only reads the abstract/conclusion would come away more confident in "robustness" than the full appendix supports.
- No repository link for the paper's *own* orchestration code (data-generation glue, hyperparameter-sweep harness) was found in the source — all *cited* implementations are public, but exact reproduction of this specific 70k-experiment pipeline would require re-implementing the harness from the paper's prose description alone.

### 跟我们的关系

Per the topic's own prior wiki entry (2510.14488, read under this same `0710-causal-scs` scope), `causal-scs-indicator` is an indicator/score project touching causal-discovery sample sufficiency and/or trustworthiness. This paper is **not itself an indicator paper** — it proposes no new score or diagnostic — but it is a highly relevant evidence/precedent source, with the exact relationship depending on where `causal-scs-indicator`'s core claim actually sits:

- **If the indicator targets "when is a differentiable causal-discovery result trustworthy given possible misspecification"**: this paper's noise-ratio argument (`r = max σ²/min σ²`, recovery guaranteed iff `r < 1+ξ/d`) is close prior art for exactly this question in the linear case — it is essentially a *post-hoc theoretical explanation*, not an estimable-from-data indicator (ξ requires knowing the true DAG's score gap, which is unobservable in practice). If `causal-scs-indicator` proposes something *computable from data alone* that approximates or bounds this identifiability condition, that is a genuine, citable delta against this paper — frame it explicitly as "makes Loh & Bühlmann's r-based guarantee operationally usable without oracle access to ξ."
- **If the indicator relates to Var-Sortability / R²-Sortability style diagnostics**: this paper leans heavily on Reisach et al.'s Var-Sortability (2021) and R²-Sortability (2024) to explain both the scale-variation failure and the unfaithful-model SortnRegress failures — those two papers, not this one, are the closest prior art for a sortability-flavored indicator; this paper is a secondary/confirming source, not the origin.
- **If the indicator is itself a new robustness *benchmark* or *evaluation protocol*** for causal discovery under misspecification: this paper is a direct and substantial competitor/baseline — any new benchmark claim needs to explain its delta against this paper's 8-scenario, 70k-experiment, oracle-hyperparameter-tuned suite, and should specifically address the oracle-tuning limitation flagged above as a design-improvement opportunity (e.g. a benchmark that scores methods *without* ground-truth-tuned hyperparameters would be a meaningful, defensible improvement over this paper's protocol).
- **Reusable regardless of exact overlap**: the eight-scenario taxonomy (confounding / measurement error / unfaithful / autoregressive / heterogeneous / scale-variant / missing / mechanism-violation) is a ready-made stress-test suite for validating any new causal-discovery-adjacent indicator; the extreme-δ appendix result is a concrete cautionary data point that any "robustness indicator" claim should be checked against (robustness claims that don't specify their operating range, as this paper's abstract arguably doesn't, can look stronger than they are).
- **Must-cite** if `causal-scs-indicator`'s scope touches differentiable-causal-discovery robustness at all — this is the largest and most recent (Oct 2025) empirical survey of exactly that question, and any proposal in this space that fails to position against it will read as an unaddressed gap to a reviewer.
