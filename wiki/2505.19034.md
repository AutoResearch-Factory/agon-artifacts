# The influence of data gaps and outliers on resilience indicators (2505.19034)

- **Year**: 2025.05
- **Authors**: Teng Liu (Beijing Normal Univ. / TU Munich, first author), Andreas Morr, Sebastian Bathiany, Lana L. Blaschke, Zhen Qian, Chan Diao, Taylor Smith, Niklas Boers (TU Munich / Potsdam Institute for Climate Impact Research, corresponding senior authors)
- **Venue**: arXiv preprint, Nature-family journal format (`sn-jnl` class, likely submitted to a Nature-branded journal)
- **Keywords**: critical slowing down, resilience indicators, early warning signals, lag-1 autocorrelation (AC1), variance indicator, missing values, outliers, MODIS vegetation indices, kurtosis, AR(1) process
- **One-liner**: Derives a closed-form dependence between AC1- and variance-based resilience indicators (driven almost entirely by the first data point) and shows missing values/outliers systematically distort their agreement in real MODIS vegetation data.

## 精华提炼

### 管线/方法

1. **Theoretical derivation**: start from the AR(1) discretization of an Ornstein–Uhlenbeck process (local resilience = linear restoring rate λ). Define λ_AC1 = log(AC1_hat) and λ_Var = ½·log(1 − σ_ε_hat²/Var[X]). Substitute the least-squares noise-variance estimator σ_ε_hat² (which itself is a function of AC1_hat) into λ_Var, yielding a closed-form functional relationship (their Eq. 4) between λ_Var and λ_AC1 that depends on series length N and on the squared, variance-normalized first and last data points (X_1², X_N²).
2. **Numerical validation of the closed form**: simulate n=10,000 AR(1) trajectories (α=e^{-2.5}, σ_ε=1, N=1000) under 3 forced initial-condition regimes — (a) X_1 ~ N(0,1) baseline, (b) X_1 = 0, (c) X_1/√Var = ±1 — and check the predicted collapse/clustering behavior in each case.
3. **Missing-value experiment**: same AR(1) generator (α=e^{-2}, n=10,000, N=1000), randomly delete a fraction r ∈ {1%, 10%, 20%} of points, recompute λ_Var/λ_AC1 on only the remaining points, and show the closed-form relationship (and its lower bound) breaks down as r grows.
4. **Real-world application**: MODIS satellite vegetation indices — NDVI, kNDVI, EVI (MOD13Q1, 250 m, 16-day), GPP (MOD17A2, 500 m, 8-day), LAI (MCD15A3H, 500 m, 4-day) — 2000–2022 (2002–2022 for LAI), pulled via Google Earth Engine, "highest quality" flagged pixels only. Stratify by MCD12Q1 land cover (10 IGBP classes), excluding any pixel with a land-cover change 2001–2021. Stratified random sample of 100,000 locations. Detrend via rolling mean + remove a 3rd-order harmonic fit for deseasoning (cross-checked with STL). Stratify further by a 2010 harmonized above-ground-biomass composite.
5. **Matched synthetic-vs-real comparison**: for each land-cover class, generate AR(1) synthetic series (α = e^{-1}) that reproduce the *same missing-value fraction* observed in that class's real MODIS data, to isolate how much of the indicator (dis)agreement is explained by missing-value fraction alone versus true ecological/dynamical differences.
6. **Outlier experiment**: inject 5% missing + 5% outliers (drawn from [μ+kσ, μ+(k+1)σ] or the mirrored negative interval), sweep k ∈ {3.0, 3.5, 4.0, 5.0, 6.0} to control kurtosis, and track how the λ_Var–λ_AC1 scatter shifts from a "balanced" pattern (mean on the 1:1 line) to a "biased" pattern (mean shifted, λ_AC1 systematically below λ_Var). Replicate the empirically observed evergreen-forest-vs-shrubland contrast with matched kurtosis/missing-fraction synthetic series.

### 关键技术细节

- Core quantities: λ_AC1 = log(AC1_hat) (lag-1-autocorrelation-based resilience estimator) and λ_Var = ½·log(1 − σ_ε_hat²/Var[X]) (variance-based estimator); both approximate the linear restoring rate λ of a discretized Ornstein–Uhlenbeck / AR(1) local-resilience model.
- Central closed-form result (Eq. 4): λ_Var = ½·log[ 1 − N/(N−1)·(1 − exp(2λ_AC1)) + (1/(N−1))·(X_1² + exp(2λ_AC1)·X_N²)/Var[X] ]. Dropping the bracketed "second term" (initial/final-point contribution) reduces this to a universal, realization-independent lower bound (Eq. 5).
- Because λ_AC1 is negative, exp(2λ_AC1)·X_N² is generally negligible relative to X_1², so nearly all of the "extra" agreement/disagreement between the two indicators for any single finite series is governed by X_1²/Var[X] — the squared, normalized value of the **first data point alone**. This holds for any gapless series regardless of the generating process, not just AR(1) (confirmed in Supp. Fig. 1 for other processes).
- As N → ∞, or when averaging over infinitely many realizations, λ_Var → λ_AC1 exactly; any single finite realization scatters around this asymptote purely because of X_1 — i.e., high indicator agreement on real data is not by itself evidence that CSD assumptions hold.
- AR(1) parameterizations differ by experiment: (α=e^{-2.5}, N=1000) for the Fig. 1 initial-condition demo; (α=e^{-2}, N=1000, n=10,000) for the missing-value (Fig. 2) and outlier (Fig. 4) sweeps; (α=e^{-1}) matched per-MODIS-class missing fraction for Fig. 3 and Fig. 5b,c.
- Missing-value mechanics: one missing value removes **2** consecutive points from the AC1 computation (both members of the affected lag pair) but only **1** point from the variance computation — this asymmetry is the mechanistic reason gaps decorrelate λ_Var from λ_AC1.
- Outlier mechanics: λ_Var stays roughly stable under outliers because both σ_ε_hat² and Var[X] inflate together (their ratio is close to outlier-invariant), whereas λ_AC1 drops because outliers directly disrupt the lag-1 correlation structure — this asymmetric sensitivity produces the "biased pattern" (λ_AC1 systematically under-reads relative to λ_Var, i.e., AC1-based resilience is over-estimated / warnings would come too late).
- Outlier magnitude is quantified via kurtosis (a standard 4th-moment tail-heaviness statistic) of the deseasoned+detrended series, not a bespoke metric.
- Empirical anchor numbers: MODIS NDVI closed shrublands ≈1.6% missing, median kurtosis 3.81 → "balanced" pattern; evergreen broadleaf forest ≈66.0% missing, median kurtosis 7.17 → "biased" pattern. This reproduces/explains the biomass-dependent indicator-agreement effect originally reported by Smith & Boers (2023, Nat. Ecol. Evol.) — the paper's explicit explanandum.
- Robustness: the missing-fraction-vs-agreement negative correlation holds across all 5 MODIS indices and is cross-checked against an alternative deseasoning pipeline (STL) with consistent results.

### 核心结果

- Eq. 4/5 confirmed numerically on gap-free AR(1) series: baseline scatter lies above a universal lower-bound curve; forcing X_1=0 collapses points onto that bound; forcing X_1/√Var=±1 clusters points on the λ_Var=λ_AC1 identity line — exactly as the closed form predicts (Fig. 1a–c).
- Missing data breaks the closed form: even r=1% missing visibly distorts the scatter relative to the gap-free case; by r=20% the three initial-condition regimes become indistinguishable and the "universal lower bound" no longer bounds the data at all (Fig. 2).
- Global MODIS pattern: high-biomass land-cover types (evergreen forest) have systematically higher missing-value fractions than low-biomass types (open shrubland), attributed to greater cloud cover over moist, high-biomass regions (vs. low cloud / low biomass in subtropical high-static-stability dry zones).
- Missing-value fraction correlates strongly negatively with λ_Var–λ_AC1 agreement across all 10 IGBP land-cover classes and all 5 vegetation indices (Fig. 3). Matched synthetic series (same missing fraction, generic AR(1) dynamics, no real ecosystem-specific behavior) closely reproduce the real agreement-vs-land-cover curve — implying missing-value fraction alone, not underlying ecological differences, is the primary driver of the biomass-dependent indicator-agreement pattern first reported by Smith & Boers (2023).
- Outliers: increasing kurtosis (via larger k) monotonically reduces λ_Var–λ_AC1 correlation and shifts the scatter mean off the 1:1 line, systematically under-reading λ_AC1 relative to λ_Var (Fig. 4) — i.e., AC1-based resilience assessment risks late warning under heavy-tailed real-world data.
- Real vegetation data reproduces this exact transition: evergreen broadleaf forest (high kurtosis + high missing %) shows the "biased" pattern; closed shrubland (low kurtosis + low missing %) shows the "balanced" pattern (Fig. 5) — matched synthetic replication (same missing%/kurtosis, generic AR(1) dynamics) reproduces both, again pointing to data quality (not ecosystem dynamics) as the driver.
- All findings hold across 5 MODIS indices, 2 native resolutions (250 m / 500 m), and 2 independent detrend/deseasoning pipelines (rolling-mean+harmonic vs. STL) — a broad internal robustness sweep for a purely observational/statistical result.

### 明确局限

- Explicitly out of scope (stated in Discussion): does not evaluate other resilience indicators beyond the AC1/variance pair (e.g., drift-function-based estimators, per Morr et al. 2024); does not disentangle missing-value *spatial/temporal pattern* from missing-value *fraction* (flagged as future work, Supp. Fig. 11); does not vary outlier *count* independent of *magnitude* (also flagged, Supp. Fig. 12); does not test alternative outlier-generating mechanisms.
- No independent ground-truth validation against actual disturbance-recovery experiments — the paper itself argues (citing Smith 2022/2023) that CSD indicators should eventually be validated against empirically measured post-perturbation recovery rates, but does not do this itself.
- Code/data explicitly deferred: "will be publicly available upon acceptance of this manuscript" — not open at preprint stage, no repo link in the tex source.
- Diagnoses the problem but proposes no fix: no new gap-filling or bias-correction method is introduced; the paper only gestures at "emerging AI-based reconstruction methods" (citing Bochow et al. 2025) as a "promising but uncertain" direction, explicitly stating their ability to preserve resilience-relevant dynamics "remains uncertain."
- Purely univariate, single-time-series, single-location analysis — no causal structure, no multivariate coupling, no graphical/causal model; the "resilience indicator" is a scalar CSD statistic computed independently per grid cell, with no spatial or cross-variable coupling considered.
- All "ground truth" in the empirical section is internal consistency between two indicators, not a labeled tipping/non-tipping event set — so the paper cannot and does not claim anything about actual predictive skill for real transitions.

### 跟我们的关系

- **No method-level collision** with `0710-causal-scs-indicator`: this paper never touches causal discovery, causal-effect estimators (no PCMCI+, no LKIF, no VAR-based causal proxy), or simulated saddle-node dynamics. It stays entirely inside the classical scalar-CSD toolbox (AC1 and variance indicators on a single univariate time series). No claim-level overlap to worry about for novelty.
- **Directly relevant as the most rigorous available account of the exact baseline our pilot benchmarks against**: our README's success gate requires "two-channel AUROC exceeds the raw-state variance/autocorrelation baseline by ≥ 0.05." This paper is the sharpest existing characterization of when and why that raw-state baseline is unreliable — indicator "agreement" is largely an artifact of the first data point, not the underlying dynamics, and is further degraded/biased by missing data and outliers. Worth citing in a related-work/motivation section as grounds for scrutinizing a purely raw-state EWS baseline, independent of whether our causal approach ultimately beats it.
- **Cautionary methodological parallel for our own two-channel design**: our dispersion channel (rolling variance/CI width) and jump channel (normalized first differences, sign flips) are conceptually adjacent to this paper's λ_Var/λ_AC1 pair — both designs combine two nominally different EWS-style statistics into one joint score. This paper's central warning — that two "different" indicators can show spurious agreement or disagreement driven by a boundary artifact (the first data point) rather than genuine shared signal — is a concrete precedent for a check we should run before trusting any joint dispersion+jump AUROC uplift: verify the two channels aren't spuriously coupled through a shared window-edge/boundary artifact rather than truly independent evidence.
- **Plausible confound for our own frozen pilot result**: our pilot's raw EWS (variance/autocorrelation) baseline scored AUROC = 0.919 on paired saddle-node simulations — surprisingly strong, and the reason our two-channel causal approach (joint AUROC 0.428) looked weak by comparison. This paper shows that real-world AC1/variance indicators are frequently degraded by missing values and outliers; our pilot is a clean, gap-free, outlier-free simulation, so this paper suggests our raw-EWS baseline may be unrealistically strong relative to what raw EWS would score on actual noisy/gappy field data — an external-validity caveat worth flagging explicitly in the next refinement round rather than concluding raw EWS "just wins."
- **Not a novelty competitor; a strong citation candidate** for two specific points: (a) motivating why raw CSD indicators are imperfect and any baseline comparison must be chosen/interpreted carefully; (b) the general principle that joint/composite EWS indicators can carry hidden statistical dependencies that masquerade as corroboration.
- **No code released yet** to reuse directly, but the closed-form Eq. 4 relationship is in principle usable as a fast analytic sanity-check: it could test whether our own dispersion-channel null-percentile calibration is contaminated by a similar first/last-data-point boundary artifact. A minor methodological borrow, not a core dependency.
